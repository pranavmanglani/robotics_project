Motion Categories

Linear Motion

Dimensions

  • One Dimensional: Pure translation along a single straight line axis (e.g., a sliding rail).
  • Two Dimensional: Movement across a planar surface, combining two axes (e.g., X and Y movement).

Analysis

Characteristics

  • Velocity vectors remain parallel.
  • No rotational change in the body's orientation.
  • Path is represented by straight lines.

Practicality

Applications

  • Gantry robot systems.
  • Telescopic actuators.
  • Pick and place Cartesian robots.

Angular Motion

Characteristics

  • Body revolves around a fixed point or axis.
  • Orientation changes relative to the frame.
  • Measured in Angular Displacement (Radians/Degrees).

Practicality

Applications

  • Rotary joints in industrial arms.
  • Camera pan/tilt mechanisms.
  • Orientation alignment for satellites.

Circular Motion

Characteristics

  • Path is a circle with a fixed radius.
  • Involves centripetal acceleration toward the center.
  • Angular velocity is constant in uniform circular motion.

Practicality

Applications

  • Wheeled robot steering and movement.
  • Scanning LiDAR sensors.
  • Pulley and belt drive systems.

Joint Architectures

Prismatic Joints

Characteristics

  • Sliding / Translational motion between links.
  • Restricts rotation entirely.

Advantages

  • High mechanical rigidity.
  • Precise linear tracking.

Revolute Joints

Characteristics

  • Rotational movement around a hinge.
  • Primary joint for articulated arms.

Advantages

  • Wide range of motion.
  • Compact and easy to actuate.

Spherical Joints

Characteristics

  • Ball-and-socket type movement.
  • Provides 3 Rotational Degrees of Freedom.

Advantages

  • High dexterity in a small space.
  • Mimics biological joint systems.

Understanding Links

Kinematic Definition

A Link (kinematic element) is defined as a single, structurally rigid body (or a collection of rigidly connected bodies) within a robot that possesses relative motion with respect to other components. Links interconnect via joints to form kinematic chains.

Structural Classifications

Rigid Links

Idealized structural bodies with negligible deformation under load[ : 38, 39].

  • High modulus of elasticity[ : 41].
  • Precision positional repeatability[ : 42].
  • Used in heavy-duty welding arms[ : 42].

Flexible Links

Designed with purposeful compliance to absorb impact or minimize weight[ : 43, 44].

  • Constructed from carbon fiber[ : 46].
  • Used in space manipulators (ISS)[ : 47].
  • Soft collaborative robots (Cobots)[ : 47].

Floating Links

Mobile links in a kinematic loop not directly grounded to the base[ : 48, 49].

  • Motion is dependent on adjacent links[ : 50].
  • Used in Stewart Platforms[ : 52].
  • Quadrupedal robot legs (Spot)[ : 52].

Degree of Freedom

Core Concepts

  • Definition: The number of independent parameters defining a robot's configuration.
  • Free Space: A body in 3D space has 6 DOF (3 linear, 3 rotational).
  • Joint Impact: Each joint reduces total DOF to provide controlled, predictable motion paths.
